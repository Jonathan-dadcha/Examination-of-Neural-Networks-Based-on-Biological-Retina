# Abstract

Background: Current artificial retinal models struggle to predict neural responses under complex natural conditions, as their accuracy degrades outside idealized laboratory environments.

Aim or Purpose: To develop an efficient biomimetic deep learning framework capable of accurately predicting biological retinal spike trains under dynamic natural scene stimulation.

Methods used: A synchronized pipeline aligned natural scene videos with biological retinal ganglion cell recordings. Four distinct paradigms were evaluated: classical generalized linear models, biophysical retinomorphic ordinary differential equations, standard spatiotemporal convolutional networks, and a novel biomimetic foveated architecture. This optimal architecture combined saliency-guided field-of-attention selection foveated spatial stream splitting, and recurrent long short-term memory layers.

Findings/results: The classical statistical and biophysical models failed to predict firing dynamics. The proposed unified foveated model achieved the highest predictive performance. (R ~ 0.33), improving accuracy over standard convolutional networks while delivering a ninety-one percent reduction in total computational operations.

Conclusion: Integrating biomimetic spatial sampling with deep recurrent networks successfully captures complex retinal processing, demonstrating that architectural efficiency can coexist with superior predictive accuracy.

Key words: Retinal modeling, foveated vision, spatiotemporal convolutions, LSTM, neural prediction, biomimetic architecture.

# Introduction

The challenge of mapping biological sensory processing to artificial computer vision is a central focus in modern engineering. The mammalian retina is not a passive digital camera sensor that uniformly captures frames. Instead, it functions as a neural circuit that processes and compresses visual information asynchronously into sparse, temporally precise spike trains \[1\]. While biological systems achieve energy efficiency by dynamically modulating spatial and temporal features based on visual statistics, standard computer vision pipelines rely on uniform spatial computations that treat every pixel across the visual field with equal weight. Developing computational frameworks that accurately model how the retina encodes dynamic natural scene inputs is necessary for advancing neuromorphic engineering, robotic vision, and retinal prosthetics.

The purpose of this project is to implement a unified evaluation platform to benchmark diverse retinal modeling paradigms under identical naturalistic stimuli, and to evaluate a deep learning framework that replicates biological neural responses. Historically, computational neuroscience and machine learning have addressed this mapping using distinct methodologies, which can be categorized into three prevailing strategies:

Biophysical Modeling (Path A): Grounded in physiological mechanisms, these architectures utilize Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and Conductance-Based Encoding Models (CBEM) to track time-varying synaptic currents and evaluate membrane potentials \[2\]. While interpretable for cellular sub-circuits under isolated laboratory conditions, they do not scale to natural scene statistics. When subjected to spatial shifts, linear averaging assumptions fail to capture synchronous burst-firing dynamics, leading to a predictive correlation of R ~ 0.02.

Retinomorphic Systems (Path B): These frameworks represent retinal layers as coupled networks of non-linear Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) solved via numerical integration methods like 4th-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) \[3\]. Although capable of achieving execution speeds on localized hardware, they operate primarily as leaky integrators or low-pass filters. Consequently, they lack the structural plasticity to adapt to spatial statistics without manual hyperparameter re-tuning and fail to replicate rapid, biphasic neural responses, yielding a correlation of R ~ 0.00.

Standard Deep Learning (Path C): Modern deep learning models utilize uniform, full-resolution spatiotemporal Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to fit the non-linear relationship between video sequences and biological spike trains \[4\]. CNNs achieve predictive accuracy by automatically extracting features directly from data, reaching a correlation of R ~ 0.29. However, their architectural design requires uniform processing of the entire visual field, demanding approximately 194.8 million MACs per frame, which restricts practical deployment.

To address these limitations, this project implements a Unified Foveated CNN architecture (Path D). This bio-inspired approach rejects uniform spatial processing, allocating high-resolution convolutional resources exclusively to a dynamic foveal center while applying space-variant subsampling and dilation to the background periphery \[5\]. By embedding a saliency-guided field-of-attention selection mechanism alongside recurrent neural layers, this framework captures temporal history while restricting computation to informative regions. This specific architectural path demonstrates that peripheral spatial downsampling can function as a mathematical regularizer. It reduces redundant background computations to decrease the computational load by 80.1% (down to 38.84 million MACs) compared to the standard spatiotemporal baseline, while maximizing the non-linear predictive accuracy to a correlation of R ~ 0.33.

# Problems and Solutions

This section analyzes the structural and numerical challenges encountered during development and documents the programmatic adjustments implemented to resolve them.

- Temporal Data Leakage in Validation Splitting:

- The Problem: Sequential time-series frames inherently possess high temporal proximity and stationarity. Implementing an unseeded frame-level shuffling technique (torch.utils.data.random_split) during baseline training causes adjacent temporal sequences to populate both the training and verification subsets simultaneously. This overlap introduces data leakage, resulting in an artificial inflation of evaluation accuracy that reflects localized temporal correlation rather than model generalization.
- The Solution: The standalone training framework was modified to deploy a continuous, block-based validation split. By partitioning index structures sequentially via subset mappings, the training iterations were restricted to an initial continuous time window, while the remaining terminal block was isolated exclusively for evaluation. This arrangement eliminates frame-level interleaving and forces validation across independent, chronologically un-seen sequence boundaries.

- Numerical Instability (NaN Loss) and Predictive Baseline Collapse:

- The Problem: The optimization pipeline executes a continuous Poisson negative log-likelihood function (nn.PoissonNLLLoss), evaluating target arrays against the internal product variable log(). Utilizing a standard Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) readout at the regression output causes negative pre-activations to undergo zero-clamping. The resulting zero-valued predictions ( = 0) generate mathematically undefined states (NaN) during log transformation, causing immediate gradient failure. Concurrently, models exposed to dynamic scenes frequently succumb to baseline collapse, minimizing loss fields by tracking a flat, constant output mean.
- The Solution: The output head was equipped with an nn.Softplus activation function to ensure smooth, strictly positive firing rate tracking (f(x) = log(1 + )), avoiding zero boundaries and preserving gradient continuity. To stabilize optimization against flatline collapse, an activity regularizer penalizing the variance between prediction and target means ( = 0.001) was added to the objective layer, while the final linear bias was seeded at a positive baseline (0.1) to anchor early parameter updates.

- Spatial Computational Footprint Strain

- The Problem: Brute-force convolutional extraction across full-resolution spatial volumes imposes a high computational load per inference frame. Processing expansive spatial inputs uniformly scales up internal feature maps and places heavy demands on runtime hardware components.
- The Solution: The processing workflow was decoupled into parallel multi-stream paths that implement spatial spatial scaling. Geometric reduction was achieved by shifting the high-resolution convolution operations exclusively to a narrow, localized foveal crop. For broader contextual coverage, the peripheral stream incorporated sequential dilated convolution layers (dilation=4 and dilation=2) to span a wide receptive field without structural downsampling, allowing the downstream recurrent core to evaluate compacted feature vectors.
